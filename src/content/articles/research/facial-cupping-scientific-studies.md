Scientific interest in cupping therapy has grown over the past decade, with an increasing number of published studies examining its physiological effects. Here we summarize key findings relevant to cupping therapy and its potential facial applications.

## Cupping and Circulation

Several studies have demonstrated increased local blood flow following cupping therapy using laser Doppler imaging and other measurement techniques. A 2021 study found that cupping significantly increased skin blood flow — with peak flow reaching 5 to 17 times baseline depending on cup size and negative pressure — and effects persisted for minutes after cup removal [\[source\]](https://doi.org/10.1111/srt.12970). A separate 2021 dose-response study confirmed that higher negative pressure (-300 mmHg) produced larger blood flow increases than lower pressure (-225 mmHg), and that shorter duration (5 min) was more effective than longer (10 min) for peak flow [\[source\]](https://doi.org/10.3389/fbioe.2020.608509). This increased perfusion is thought to promote oxygenation and nutrient delivery to skin tissues, which is the primary mechanism cited for potential cosmetic benefits.

However, most circulation studies use body-cupping suction levels (typically -225 to -300 mmHg), which are considerably stronger than the gentle suction used in facial cupping (-50 to -100 mmHg). Whether the circulation-boosting effects scale proportionally at lower pressures remains an open question [\[source\]](https://doi.org/10.3233/BMR-200120).

## Cupping and Pain Management

Systematic reviews have found moderate evidence for cupping in managing chronic neck and back pain conditions. A 2020 meta-analysis of 18 randomized trials (1,172 participants) concluded that cupping therapy produced large short-term effects on pain intensity compared to no treatment, though effects were not significant when compared to sham cupping [\[source\]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpain.2020.01.002). A 2018 systematic review in BMJ Open similarly found cupping reduced neck pain compared to no intervention or active controls, but the authors noted that blinding is difficult in cupping studies, which introduces potential bias [\[source\]](https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/8/11/e021070).

For facial applications, the pain management research is indirectly relevant. Facial tension headaches, TMJ discomfort, and sinus pressure are conditions where practitioners report positive outcomes, but dedicated facial cupping trials are still limited.

## Limitations of Current Evidence

It is important to be transparent about where the evidence stands. Most studies examine body cupping with stronger suction than facial cupping uses. Direct application of these findings to facial cosmetic outcomes requires caution. The available research on cosmetic facial cupping specifically is limited to small pilot studies and anecdotal reports. Larger, well-controlled trials with standardized protocols are needed before definitive claims can be made.
