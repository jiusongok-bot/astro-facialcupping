Scientific interest in cupping therapy has grown over the past decade, with an increasing number of published studies examining its physiological effects. Here we summarize key findings relevant to cupping therapy and its potential facial applications.

## Cupping and Circulation

Several studies have demonstrated increased local blood flow following cupping therapy using laser Doppler imaging and other measurement techniques. A 2021 randomized controlled trial found that a single cupping session significantly increased cutaneous blood flow in the treated area, with effects persisting up to 30 minutes after cup removal. This increased perfusion is thought to promote oxygenation and nutrient delivery to skin tissues, which is the primary mechanism cited for potential cosmetic benefits.

However, most circulation studies use body-cupping suction levels (typically -225 to -300 mmHg), which are considerably stronger than the gentle suction used in facial cupping (-50 to -100 mmHg). Whether the circulation-boosting effects scale proportionally at lower pressures remains an open question.

## Cupping and Pain Management

Systematic reviews have found moderate evidence for cupping in managing chronic neck and back pain conditions. A 2023 meta-analysis of 17 randomized trials concluded that cupping therapy was superior to usual care for pain reduction, though the authors noted that blinding is difficult in cupping studies, which introduces potential bias.

For facial applications, the pain management research is indirectly relevant. Facial tension headaches, TMJ discomfort, and sinus pressure are conditions where practitioners report positive outcomes, but dedicated facial cupping trials are still limited.

## Limitations of Current Evidence

It is important to be transparent about where the evidence stands. Most studies examine body cupping with stronger suction than facial cupping uses. Direct application of these findings to facial cosmetic outcomes requires caution. The available research on cosmetic facial cupping specifically is limited to small pilot studies and anecdotal reports. Larger, well-controlled trials with standardized protocols are needed before definitive claims can be made.
