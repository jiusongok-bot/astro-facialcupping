A common concern about facial cupping is whether it can damage the skin. When practiced correctly, facial cupping is generally safe — but improper technique can cause problems.

## When It's Safe

Facial cupping is safe when:
- Using appropriate (light to medium) suction
- Keeping cups in constant motion
- Using adequate lubrication
- Using cups specifically designed for facial use
- Avoiding broken, irritated, or infected skin

## Potential Risks of Improper Use

### Bruising
The most common issue. Bruising occurs when suction is too strong, cups are left stationary, or insufficient oil is used. Unlike body cupping, facial cupping should not leave marks.

### Broken Capillaries
Aggressive or repetitive cupping on the same area can cause capillary damage, resulting in small red or purple spots (petechiae). These typically heal on their own.

### Skin Irritation
Dragging cups on dry skin (without enough oil) can cause friction burns or irritation. Always use a generous amount of facial oil.

### Temporary Redness
Mild redness immediately after cupping is normal and should resolve within minutes. Persistent redness may indicate too much suction.

## How to Practice Safely

1. Start with very light suction
2. Use plenty of facial oil
3. Keep cups moving at all times
4. Limit sessions to 5-10 minutes
5. Stop if you feel pain (not just pressure)

## When to Avoid Facial Cupping

Do not use facial cupping on:
- Active acne or breakouts
- Sunburned or windburned skin
- Open wounds or cuts
- Severe rosacea flare-ups
- Areas with suspicious moles or lesions

## The Bottom Line

The risk of skin damage from facial cupping is low when proper technique is used. The vast majority of reported issues result from excessive suction, inadequate lubrication, or using body cups on the face.
