Facial cupping is not suitable for everyone. Understanding the contraindications — both absolute and relative — helps you make an informed decision about whether this practice is right for you.

## Absolute Contraindications

These conditions mean you should not practice facial cupping under any circumstances.

**Active skin infections or open wounds:** Acne with active pustules, cold sores (herpes simplex), impetigo, or any open cuts, sores, or abrasions on the face are absolute contraindications. Cupping can spread bacteria or viruses across the face and worsen the infection. Wait until skin is fully healed before attempting facial cupping.

**Severe rosacea or eczema flare-ups:** The mechanical action of cupping can aggravate these inflammatory conditions, causing increased redness, irritation, and flare intensity. Those with mild, well-managed rosacea may be able to cup with extreme caution, but active flare-ups require avoidance.

**Blood clotting disorders:** Conditions such as hemophilia, von Willebrand disease, or thrombocytopenia increase the risk of severe bruising and subcutaneous bleeding. If you have any clotting disorder, do not practice facial cupping.

**Recent facial surgery or procedures:** Wait at least 4-6 weeks after any facial surgery (facelift, blepharoplasty, rhinoplasty) or injectable treatments (fillers, Botox) before considering cupping. Cupping can interfere with healing and may displace fillers.

## Relative Contraindications (Consult First)

These conditions do not rule out cupping entirely but require caution and preferably professional guidance.

**Sensitive or reactive skin:** If your skin frequently reacts to new products or environmental changes, start with the lightest possible suction on a small test area. Limit sessions to 2-3 minutes and watch for adverse reactions over the next 24 hours. If no irritation occurs, you can gradually extend session time. Avoid cupping on days when your skin is already feeling reactive or irritated from other factors like weather, diet, or skincare products.

**History of broken capillaries:** If you are prone to broken capillaries (common on fair or thin skin), use only the smallest cups on the lowest suction setting. Avoid the delicate under-eye area and sides of the nose entirely. Consider skipping facial cupping altogether if you have a known tendency to develop spider veins or have a family history of weak capillary walls.

**Pregnancy:** Hormonal changes during pregnancy can increase skin sensitivity and fragility. A conservative approach is to avoid cupping during the first trimester and proceed with very light pressure only after consulting a healthcare provider.

**Use of blood-thinning medications:** Prescription blood thinners (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban) and even over-the-counter options like aspirin or high-dose fish oil can increase bruising risk. If you take any blood-thinning medication, use minimal suction and keep sessions short, or avoid cupping altogether.
