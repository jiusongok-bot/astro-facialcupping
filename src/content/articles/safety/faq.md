## Can facial cupping damage skin?

When done correctly with proper technique and light suction, facial cupping should not damage skin. The skin is remarkably resilient, and the gentle suction used in facial cupping is well within its tolerance range. Damage typically results from excessive suction, inadequate lubrication, or leaving cups stationary for too long. Signs of potential damage include persistent redness lasting more than an hour, broken capillaries, bruising, or skin irritation. If you experience any of these, take a break from cupping until your skin fully recovers, then resume with lighter suction and shorter sessions. Using cups specifically designed for the face — not body cups — and keeping them in constant motion are your best safeguards against skin damage.

It is also worth noting that pre-existing skin conditions, such as active acne or sunburn, can increase vulnerability to damage even with correct technique. Always ensure your skin is in a healthy, calm state before cupping. If you have concerns about your skin's resilience, a patch test on a small area like the jawline can help you gauge how your skin responds before committing to a full session.

## Can you do facial cupping every day?

Yes, with light suction and proper technique, daily facial cupping is safe and many practitioners recommend it. A daily 5-10 minute session is sufficient to maintain lymphatic drainage and circulation benefits. However, it is important to listen to your skin. If you notice any sensitivity, redness that lingers, or tenderness, reduce frequency to every other day or three times per week until your skin adjusts. Some users prefer to alternate between a full facial cupping routine one day and a focused routine (targeting only specific areas like the jawline or under-eye) the next. The key is consistency without overworking any one area. If you are new to cupping, start with 2-3 sessions per week for the first two weeks before increasing frequency.

## Is facial cupping safe during pregnancy?

This is a question best directed to your healthcare provider, as every pregnancy is different. Pregnancy causes significant hormonal changes that can make skin more sensitive, reactive, and prone to broken capillaries. Many estheticians advise avoiding facial cupping during the first trimester as a precaution. During the second and third trimesters, some women continue cupping with very light pressure and no issues, while others find their skin too sensitive. A patch test on a small area (such as the jawline) can help gauge your skin's tolerance. If you have pregnancy-related conditions such as high blood pressure or preeclampsia, it is generally recommended to avoid cupping entirely. Always consult your OB-GYN or midwife before incorporating any new skincare practice during pregnancy.
