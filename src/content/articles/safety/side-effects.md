When done correctly, facial cupping has minimal side effects. However, being aware of what can occur — and why — helps you practice safely and recognize when something is wrong.

## Common (Mild)

**Temporary redness** is the most common side effect and is completely normal. The suction brings blood to the surface of the skin, which can cause a flushed appearance that fades within 5 to 30 minutes after your session. This response is actually a sign of increased circulation and is generally considered a positive outcome.

**Mild tenderness** after the first few sessions is also common, particularly if you have sensitive skin or are not used to the sensation of cupping. This tenderness typically diminishes as your skin adapts to the practice. Starting with very light suction and shorter sessions (2-3 minutes) can help minimize this discomfort.

## Possible (If Done Incorrectly)

**Bruising** occurs when too much suction is applied or when cups remain stationary on the skin for too long. Unlike body cupping where marks are intentional, facial cupping should never leave marks. If you notice bruising, reduce suction pressure and keep the cups moving continuously.

**Broken capillaries** (telangiectasia) can result from aggressive technique or excessive suction on delicate areas. These tiny, visible blood vessels are more common around the nose and cheeks. Using the smallest cups with the lightest suction on these areas is essential. Once broken capillaries appear, they may require professional treatment such as laser therapy to resolve, so prevention is far preferable.

**Skin irritation** often stems from inadequate lubrication. Without enough oil, the cup drags rather than glides, creating friction that can cause redness, rawness, or even minor abrasions. Always apply a generous layer of facial oil before starting, and reapply if the cup begins to drag. Irritation can also occur if cups are not cleaned properly between uses, so wash your cups with warm soapy water after each session and allow them to air dry completely.

**Allergic reactions** to the cup material or your chosen facial oil are rare but possible. Silicone allergies are uncommon, but if you have a known sensitivity, look for hypoallergenic or latex-free options. Always patch test a new oil on a small area of skin before using it during a cupping session.

## When to Stop

If you experience persistent pain (not just mild tenderness), significant bruising, broken capillaries that do not resolve within a few days, or any skin changes such as blistering or peeling, discontinue use immediately. These signs indicate that your technique needs adjustment or that facial cupping may not be suitable for your skin type. Consult a healthcare provider or licensed esthetician before resuming.
