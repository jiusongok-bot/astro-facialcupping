Cupping therapy has been practiced for over 3,000 years across multiple civilizations. While facial cupping is a relatively modern adaptation, the principles behind it draw from ancient traditions spanning several continents.

![Traditional massage therapy](/images/articles/massage-therapy.jpg)

## Ancient Origins

**Egypt (circa 1550 BCE):** The earliest recorded use of cupping appears in the Ebers Papyrus, an ancient Egyptian medical document. Egyptian healers used cupping to treat fever, pain, and menstrual imbalances using hollowed animal horns or bronze cups.

**China (circa 300 BCE):** Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) adopted cupping as a core modality alongside acupuncture and moxibustion. The earliest TCM texts describe cupping as a method to balance qi (life energy) and remove stagnation from the body's meridians. Chinese practitioners used bamboo cups heated with fire to create suction — a technique still practiced today as fire cupping.

**Greece and Rome (circa 400 BCE):** Hippocrates, the father of Western medicine, wrote extensively about cupping. Greek and Roman physicians used cupping for respiratory conditions, musculoskeletal pain, and menstrual disorders. The cups were typically made of bronze or glass.

**Middle East (circa 600 CE):** Islamic scholars preserved and expanded Greco-Roman medical knowledge, including cupping. The practice, known as hijama, became an important therapeutic method in Islamic medicine and remains widely practiced today.

## The Transition to Facial Use

For most of its history, cupping was applied to the back, shoulders, and larger muscle groups. The adaptation to facial use began in earnest during the 2010s, driven by several converging trends:

The rise of non-invasive beauty treatments created demand for tools that offered visible results without surgery or injections. Korean beauty trends, which emphasized gentle, massage-based skincare, popularized facial cupping as part of daily routines. And manufacturers began producing silicone cups specifically designed for facial contours — softer, smaller, and gentler than traditional glass or bamboo cups.

## Modern Recognition

Facial cupping gained mainstream attention around 2015-2017 as celebrities and estheticians shared their routines on social media. Today it is offered in spas worldwide, recommended by dermatologists as a complementary treatment, and practiced by millions at home.

The practice continues to evolve. Recent innovations include electric cupping devices with adjustable pressure, combination tools that pair cupping with microcurrent, and specialized cup shapes designed for specific facial zones.
